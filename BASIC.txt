WAREHOUSE MANAGEMENT SYSTEM - BASIC CONCEPTS

--------------------------------------------------

1. WAREHOUSE
A warehouse is a place where goods and products are stored before they are distributed to customers, retailers, or other locations.

Real-world example:
Amazon stores thousands of products in large warehouses before delivering them to customers after an order is placed.

--------------------------------------------------

2. WAREHOUSE MANAGEMENT SYSTEM (WMS)
A Warehouse Management System is software that helps manage warehouse operations such as inventory tracking, order processing, and stock movement.

Real-world example:
When you order a product online, the system automatically checks stock availability, locates the item, and processes shipping. This is handled by a WMS.

--------------------------------------------------

3. LEGACY SYSTEM
A legacy system is an old or outdated system that is still in use but may not be efficient or modern.

Real-world example:
A warehouse using paper records or Excel sheets to track inventory instead of software is using a legacy system.

--------------------------------------------------

4. ADVANTAGE OF WAREHOUSE OVER LEGACY SYSTEM
Modern warehouse systems (WMS) are faster, more accurate, and automated compared to legacy systems.

Advantages:
- Real-time inventory tracking
- Reduced human errors
- Faster order processing
- Better data management

Real-world example:
Instead of manually checking stock in registers, a WMS shows stock instantly on a computer, saving time and reducing mistakes.

--------------------------------------------------

5. INVENTORY
Inventory refers to the goods and products stored in a warehouse that are ready for sale or distribution.

Real-world example:
In a supermarket warehouse, items like rice, oil, and snacks stored in bulk are considered inventory.

--------------------------------------------------

6. YARD
A yard is the area outside the warehouse where trucks and vehicles wait before loading or unloading goods.

Real-world example:
Delivery trucks arriving at a warehouse wait in the yard until they are assigned a dock door for unloading.

--------------------------------------------------

7. DOCK DOOR
A dock door is an entry/exit point in a warehouse where goods are loaded into or unloaded from trucks.

Real-world example:
A truck carrying goods backs up to a dock door, and workers unload boxes directly into the warehouse.

--------------------------------------------------

8. INBOUND OPERATION
Inbound operations involve receiving goods into the warehouse from suppliers.

Steps include:
- Receiving goods
- Checking quality and quantity
- Storing items in proper locations

Real-world example:
A truck delivers new stock to a warehouse, workers scan and store items on shelves. This is inbound.

--------------------------------------------------

9. OUTBOUND OPERATION
Outbound operations involve sending goods from the warehouse to customers or stores.

Steps include:
- Picking items from storage
- Packing orders
- Shipping to customers

Real-world example:
When a customer places an online order, workers pick the item, pack it, and ship it. This is outbound.

--------------------------------------------------
